Some parts of the climate system could cross a threshold, or "tipping point", beyond which change becomes self-sustaining and hard to reverse. Knowing how close we are to such thresholds — and whether early warning signals can reliably be detected before they are crossed — is one of the most pressing questions in climate science.

Through **Project VERIFY**, we are helping to build an early warning system for climate tipping points. The project is led by [David Thornalley](https://profiles.ucl.ac.uk/13327-david-thornalley), co-led with Dr Ruza Ivanovic at the University of Leeds, and brings together paleoclimate reconstructions, observations, statistics and artificial intelligence. We use "Digital Twins" — highly detailed computer simulations trained on real-world data — to test how reliable candidate early warning signals really are, focusing on the Greenland Ice Sheet and the North Atlantic Subpolar Gyre. Isaac Foreman joined the group as a PhD student on this project.

VERIFY is funded by the [Advanced Research and Invention Agency (ARIA)](https://www.aria.org.uk/) with around £5 million, as part of ARIA's £81 million [Forecasting Tipping Points](https://aria.org.uk/forecasting-tipping-points/) programme. You can read more about the award in the [UCL news announcement](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/social-historical-sciences/news/2025/feb/major-grant-improve-climate-tipping-point-forecasting).
